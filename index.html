Researcher Guide: Transcribing J.P. Harrington's Notes

Who was John Peabody Harrington?

John Peabody Harrington (1884–1961) was an American linguist and ethnologist who spent his life frantically documenting the languages and oral histories of Native Americans, especially in California. His notes are legendary for three things: their immense historical value, their chaotic state (over 1 million pages written on napkins, envelopes, scraps, and legal paper), and his **extremely difficult, hurried cursive handwriting**.

Deciphering Harrington's Hand & Orthography

To record sounds exactly, Harrington created a custom, fluid phonetic orthography based on his own modifications of European characters:

  • Glottal Stop (ˀ): Harrington often wrote this as a superscript question mark, a hook, or a curly apostrophe to denote the glottal closure in languages like Chumash.
  • Lateral Fricative (ł): Denotes a voiceless lateral fricative (similar to Welsh 'll'). It looks like a standard letter 'l' with a hurried crossbar through it.
  • Barred i (ɨ): Represents a high-central unrounded vowel, typical of Californian languages. Written as an 'i' with a cross stroke.
  • Schwa (ə): Written as an inverted 'e' or a figure resembling a rounded number '3' in cursive.
  • Voiced/Voiceless Accents (ṣ, ñ, etc.): Harrington heavily used diacritical marks including subdots, macrons for long vowels, and acute accents for stress.

How "In-Context AI Training" Works

Large Multimodal Models like Gemini 1.5 Pro have deep visual-textual reasoning. They do not just read text; they analyze image composition. By providing:

  1. A Custom Glossary: Mapping Harrington's handwriting quirks to specific Unicode symbols.
  2. Few-Shot Exemplars: Giving the model 1–3 reference images and their correct, verified human transcriptions.

The AI will align the visual shapes in your new, untranscribed scans with the reference cards, adapting in real-time to Harrington's hand and your preferred transcription conventions without costly weight fine-tuning.
